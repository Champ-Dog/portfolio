One Thing at a Time

Cameron Tape

April 2020


One of the more common mistakes I've seen baristas make is hastily changing several variables when dialling in or correcting a brew. I was often told "grind finer, and tamp lighter" and similar when I was taught to make coffee. "Grind coarser and change the dose" was a go-to for a more recent colleague. I've seen these responses in every workplace and class I've been in; from both new and experienced baristas. I do it myself, more often than I care to admit.


The problem with this approach is that you won't know which change you made did what. Even if you get the result you wanted, you can't be sure why. I know first-hand how confusing this can be when you're new. I spent years confused, uncertain, and seeing coffee as some kind of dark magic that I couldn't master. I was instructed to change several things, so I did. This made it hard to understand what any of these variables had an impact on; or why changes to different variables could often lead to similar results in the cup.


It took years for this to change for me. A friend taught me a system based around standardising my dose (or at least an approximation, in a time before automatic grinders or scales were commonplace). For the first time, I was able to produce predictable results. By keeping one variable (relatively) consistent, I could respond to changes in a way that felt more rational and controlled.


I improved more as a barista in the following months than I had in the preceding years. Problems I would struggle with for hours, and never really understand, could be resolved in minutes. If a change didn't work, I knew what I had changed and could change it back. I could start to notice that changing a particular variable a certain way would typically have a similar outcome. I started to understand, and actually learn.


That was all years ago, of course. This is important when you're learning; but you and I are old hands at this. We can take shortcuts. We're experienced, we have a bunch of contextual information - we know this coffee, this equipment, what our customers like. We really can save a lot of time and energy like this! Still, when I'm feeling confused, stressed, or approaching a new coffee, I'll often remind myself - one thing at a time.


What I’ve Learned from Manual Routing

Cameron Tape

May 2022


I’m currently enrolled in a web development course. The most recent assessment task was to create a two-sided marketplace application using Ruby on Rails. We covered Rails in class, I’d read a bunch of guides, and made some simpler practice apps. I was good to go!

There wasn’t a lot of time for this assignment, so I made use of Rails’ resources routing shortcut for Profiles, and again for nesting Addresses in Profiles. My routes looked like:


                    resources :profiles do
                      resources :addresses
                    end

This was working okay for me, but I was running into a few problems with links that I didn’t really understand. I also needed to use Devise for User accounts (we’d been strongly encouraged to introduce Profiles separately rather than expanding Devise’s User class to avoid breaking anything needed for security). I wanted the Profile information to be nested in the User sign-up; and went straight back to what I knew:


                        resources :users do
                          resources :profiles do
                            resources :addresses
                          end
                        end

Every other link and button in my app broke. When I fixed one problem, another would arise — usually for the same link! I spent days trying to fix this; but I couldn’t make any headway. I eventually reached out for help to an instructor — I was halfway through my assessment period and wasn’t much further along than day one!


My instructor spent about two minutes looking at my code and sent back a screenshot of my routes, asking “Do you understand what’s going on here?” He suggested I re-write all my routes manually. I was predictably demoralised: I’d already lost a lot of time trying to solve problems, now I had to go back re-do work in a way that would take even longer.


This ended up being a remarkably positive experience! Besides eliminating a huge chunk of unnecessary routes, it helped me sort out all the problems I was having. A lot of my issues with links not working (or displaying) were from expecting the wrong params. I knew how to use params, of course; but I didn’t really understand params. Constructing my routes manually forced me to think about what params I was passing, and when, and how I wanted to use them.


Using the resources shortcuts, and nesting them as I had, meant my paths were often convoluted, and passing along more information than was necessary. I hadn’t really understood this, and as someone super fresh at Rails, I couldn’t manage it properly to get the information I needed. I hadn’t understood that this was something I controlled.


Even though it took a extra time that I didn’t feel I had, I learned a lot in the day or two it took to re-work my app. It gave me a chance to cement my understanding of routes, and to begin to understand params. I was able to fix the recurring problems I was having and solve future issues faster.


More importantly, it reminded me that the point of what I was doing was to learn, even if it took me a bit longer. Trying to learn unfamiliar skills can be daunting, but small wins like this are moments of respite that help me keep going.
